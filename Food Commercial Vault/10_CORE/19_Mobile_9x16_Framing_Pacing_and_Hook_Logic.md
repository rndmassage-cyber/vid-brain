# Mobile 9x16 Framing, Pacing and Hook Logic

## Why This Matters
A premium food video can fail on a phone if the viewer cannot read the key action instantly.  
Vertical ads need stronger visual hierarchy and faster clarity.

## Non-Negotiable Rules
- Deliver a hook in the first 1–3 seconds.
- Keep the main food subject readable on a small screen.
- Prefer one dominant action per beat.
- Avoid visual clutter near the top and bottom edges.
- Ensure the dish remains centered enough to survive platform overlays and cropping.
- Show the best result early enough to keep attention.

## First 1–3 Seconds Rule
The opening should make the viewer stop immediately.
Best vertical hooks:
- macro steam burst
- dramatic cheese stretch
- sauce impact
- crisp crack
- glossy close-up with motion
- bold ingredient drop
- spoon lift with visible thickness

Avoid slow wide introductions in vertical.

## Safe Framing for Vertical
- keep the hero action near the center band
- avoid tiny side details that require wide horizontal reading
- use bold silhouettes
- separate foreground from background strongly
- make hand interactions large enough to read immediately

## Result-First Openings
Vertical ads often benefit from showing:
- the most appetizing texture first
- the finished result before process
- the payoff clue before the explanation

Then the sequence can reveal how that result happens.

## Macro Hook Strategy
Macro works exceptionally well in vertical because it:
- fills the frame
- creates immediate tactile impact
- removes irrelevant background
- emphasizes texture over geography

But every macro opening still needs one clear subject.

## Readability on Small Screens
Readability improves when:
- the background is simple
- the vessel edge is visible enough to understand the dish
- the color contrast is strong
- the camera path is not overly complex
- the action stays in frame during the full beat

## Pace Density
Short vertical ads can feel dense, but not every shot should be frantic.
Good density:
- strong opening
- clear middle action
- memorable payoff
- minimal dead time

Too much density creates confusion rather than energy.

## When to Avoid Overcrowding
Avoid adding:
- multiple props
- too many hands
- several simultaneous actions
- decorative side ingredients that dilute the hero dish
- long lateral moves that hide the subject near the edges

## What Feels Native to Vertical
- strong center composition
- bold texture fills
- close human-food interaction
- direct, compact storytelling
- quick escalation
- loopable or sharply resolved endings

## What Feels Like Cropped TVC
- horizontal compositions merely cut into vertical
- tiny food in a large set
- slow scenic introductions
- action occurring too close to the edges
- multiple secondary objects stealing space from the hero dish
