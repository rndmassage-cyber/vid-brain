# Set Presentation, Plateware and Environment

## Why This Matters
Food never exists in a vacuum, even when the background is minimal.  
The vessel, surface, and environment either elevate the dish or make it feel cheap.

## Non-Negotiable Rules
- The dish is always the hero.
- Plateware must support the food identity.
- Props must be subordinate, not competitive.
- The environment must match the food mood.
- Surface materials should enhance appetite, not distract from it.
- If a real restaurant feel is used, the food lighting must belong to that world.

## Studio Tabletop vs Restaurant Context
### Studio tabletop
Best when:
- texture and physics are the main event
- the ad needs abstraction
- high-energy ingredient motion matters
- the environment should not distract

Traits:
- controlled surfaces
- limited props
- bold shadows
- high readability

### Restaurant context
Best when:
- atmosphere helps sell the dish
- the brand identity is hospitality-driven
- lifestyle cues matter
- the final payoff benefits from belonging to a world

Traits:
- blurred background life
- practical light sources
- believable depth
- richer emotional context

## Plateware Selection Logic
Plateware should:
- fit the cuisine and price point
- frame the food cleanly
- preserve appetite access
- contrast enough with the food to keep edges readable
- feel deliberate in material and finish

General guidance:
- burgers and sandwiches often benefit from boards, trays, wraps, or understated plates
- soups and noodles need bowls whose rim height supports steam and spoon logic
- sushi benefits from minimal, precise vessels
- desserts and drinks often benefit from reflective or elegant surfaces, but only if reflections remain controlled

## Surface and Material Hierarchy
The surface carries the set if the background is simple.
Preferred surface traits:
- dark stone
- clean ceramic
- brushed metal
- textured wood with discipline
- premium neutral surfaces

Avoid:
- loud patterns
- fake luxury textures that overpower the food
- messy surfaces with unrelated crumbs or stains

## Scale Cues
The set should tell the viewer how large and indulgent the food feels.
Scale cues can come from:
- cutlery
- hand interaction
- vessel size
- table-edge perspective
- relationship between garnish and main item

Use scale intentionally. Do not shrink the dish by surrounding it with oversized props.

## Prop Restraint
Props should answer only one of these:
- flavor context
- serving context
- brand context
- action support

If a prop does none of these, remove it.

## Background Life
When using restaurant context, the background can help if it is:
- blurred
- warm or atmosphere-driven
- visually subordinate
- lightly alive with motion, glow, or depth

Background life should support mood, not compete for attention.

## Food-to-Environment Matching
Examples:
- a premium steak dish can support moody dark ambience
- fresh salads and cold bowls usually want cleaner, brighter, fresher environments
- dessert or beverage glamour can tolerate more reflective styling
- rustic baking can support tactile surfaces and softer atmospheric warmth

## What Makes It Look Expensive
- disciplined prop economy
- vessel matched to dish identity
- strong surface choice
- clear separation from background
- restaurant context that feels cinematic, not documentary
- set elements that reinforce appetite and brand tone

## What Makes It Feel Staged or Cheap
- mismatched plateware
- too many garnish props around the dish
- surface clutter
- background details that pull the eye
- a luxury-looking table with a low-end serving vessel, or the reverse
- environmental color fighting the food palette

## Continuity Risks
- plate changes across cuts
- board grain or surface material changes suddenly
- napkin, wrapper, or utensil position resets illogically
- restaurant ambience changes color temperature mid-sequence
