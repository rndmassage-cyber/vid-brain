# 15s Commercial Architecture

## Why This Matters
Food is naturally static. A premium commercial creates desire by turning a static subject into a sequence of escalating appetitive events.
A short ad feels expensive when each shot does a different job with clear intent.

## Non-Negotiable Rules
- Start with a clear hook in the first 1–2 seconds.
- Give each shot one dominant function.
- Do not repeat the same angle, action, and scale back-to-back.
- Move from curiosity to certainty: the viewer should first feel intrigue, then hunger, then reward.
- End on a satisfying hero payoff, not on a random process fragment.
- Build the edit around contrast: tight vs wide, fast vs slow, glossy vs textured, whole dish vs detail.

## Core Sequence
### 1. Hook
The opening must stop the scroll.
Good hooks:
- violent macro texture
- steam bursting through backlight
- sauce impact
- crisp fracture
- cheese stretch starting
- knife or cutter entering frame
- a top bun, garnish, or ingredient landing with force

Bad hooks:
- passive wide shot
- generic slow pan over a finished plate
- too many objects competing for attention

### 2. Crave / Reveal
Now prove why the food deserves attention.
Use this section to show:
- premium ingredients
- freshness
- heat
- gloss
- texture separation
- read-to-eat cues

This is where the ad shifts from "interesting" to "desirable."

### 3. Interaction / Physics
This is usually the most satisfying part.
It should contain one of:
- compression
- lift
- pull
- slice
- pour
- crack
- toss
- dip
- scoop
- bite reveal

Interaction makes the food feel tangible.

### 4. Hero Payoff
The ending must show the dish in its most convincing, saleable state.
A good payoff:
- resolves visual uncertainty
- shows the full dish or the most persuasive portion of it
- preserves the strongest textures built earlier
- feels complete, not interrupted

## One Shot = One Function Rule
A shot should primarily do one of these:
- Hook
- Reveal
- Process
- Interaction
- Payoff

If a shot tries to do all of them, it becomes noisy and weak.

## Macro-to-Wide Escalation
A reliable pattern is:
1. impossibly close hook
2. slightly wider texture or ingredient reveal
3. tactile action
4. composed hero image

This progression makes the food feel monumental first, then understandable, then desirable.

## Process vs Result Balance
Food ads often fail when they lean too hard into process.  
Process is useful only when it increases craving.

Use process when it reveals:
- heat
- craftsmanship
- freshness
- tension
- structural change

Avoid process when it merely explains how cooking works.

## Rhythm and Contrast
Premium rhythm usually comes from alternating:
- stillness and impact
- soft movement and sudden force
- whole-dish recognition and microscopic detail
- fast setup and slow payoff

The sequence should feel designed, not randomly shuffled.

## What Makes It Look Expensive
- clean shot function separation
- escalation of desire
- decisive opening
- tactile middle
- memorable ending
- visual contrast without confusion
- actions timed to the strongest physical moment

## What Makes It Feel Generic
- same camera move repeated
- endless pretty but passive close-ups
- no clear hero moment
- too many ingredients with no hierarchy
- process shots that do not increase appetite
- ending before the viewer feels rewarded

## Recommended Shot Function Patterns
### Pattern A: universal premium ad
1. Hook
2. Ingredient reveal
3. Interaction
4. Hero payoff

### Pattern B: process-heavy ad
1. Hook
2. Process
3. Interaction
4. Hero payoff

### Pattern C: loopable social ad
1. Hook
2. Crave detail
3. Interaction
4. Hero image that visually returns to opening energy

## Continuity Risks
- the hero dish in the payoff no longer matches the dish teased earlier
- interaction damages the food but the damage disappears later
- process shots introduce a different plate, tool, or garnish logic than the payoff
