# Food Action, Physics and Human Interaction

## Why This Matters
The fastest way to make food feel irresistible is to make its texture and weight feel physically believable.

## Non-Negotiable Rules
- Every action must have a tactile purpose.
- Pressure must match the food texture.
- Aftermath must remain visible if the action is irreversible.
- Liquids must behave according to thickness.
- Hands and utensils must interact with the food convincingly.
- The result of the action matters as much as the action itself.

## Compression Actions
Examples:
- burger squish
- sandwich press
- mochi or pastry give
- soft bun indentation
- cream compression

Good compression:
- yields slightly before resisting
- releases sauce or juices at the edges
- preserves structure enough to stay attractive

Bad compression:
- total collapse
- no visible response
- physics that look rubbery or weightless

## Pull and Stretch Actions
Examples:
- cheese pull
- noodle lift
- caramel stretch
- pulled meat separation
- sticky glaze trail

Good stretch:
- starts thick
- narrows under tension
- catches light
- snaps or releases naturally
- maintains a believable origin point

Bad stretch:
- starts from nowhere
- becomes plastic-looking string
- never changes thickness
- breaks in a visually dead way

## Slice and Break Actions
Examples:
- pizza wheel cut
- dessert spoon crack
- sandwich cut-through
- fried shell break
- crust fracture

Good slice or break:
- creates debris or texture reveal
- shows resistance before release
- leaves a readable aftermath
- exposes an interior that contrasts with the exterior

Bad slice or break:
- no resistance
- object heals in later shots
- interior texture is mushy or undefined

## Dip, Drizzle, and Pour Actions
Examples:
- syrup pour
- sauce coat
- glaze finish
- soy-sauce dip
- spooned dressing

Good liquid behavior:
- thickness is clear
- flow is directed and elegant
- reflections travel with the liquid
- liquid accumulates somewhere real
- the source loses volume if shown

Bad liquid behavior:
- every sauce looks equally thin
- flow direction changes magically
- liquid vanishes after landing
- the surface never reacts

## Lift and Reveal Actions
Examples:
- spoon lifting soup
- chopsticks lifting noodles
- fork lifting salad bite
- slice lifted from pie or pizza
- burger lid lifted to reveal layers

Good lift:
- clarifies what the viewer wants to eat
- shows underside logic
- creates drips, strands, crumbs, or steam when appropriate
- leaves a believable gap behind

## Toss, Drop, and Splash Actions
Examples:
- wok toss
- garnish rain
- fry drop
- ice cube impact
- berry or fruit hit into cream

Good impact:
- reacts to gravity and weight
- sends material outward according to the surface
- has a readable peak moment
- leaves aftermath

Bad impact:
- frozen chaos with no focal point
- no displacement
- identical motion on objects of different weights

## Pressure Release and Aftermath
After the action, show consequence:
- compressed bun slowly rises slightly
- sauce reaches the edge and settles
- crumbs land
- steam escapes
- broth ripples
- slice gap remains
- glaze clings and stops

This is where appetite often becomes strongest.

## What Makes It Feel Tactile
- resistance before release
- clear thickness
- believable gravity
- visible pressure
- textural contrast between outside and inside
- aftermath that proves the action happened

## What Makes It Feel Weightless or Fake
- no reaction to touch
- wrong pressure for the material
- no edge deformation
- liquids with no viscosity identity
- actions with no consequence

## Continuity Risks
- bite marks disappear
- slice gaps close
- noodle origin point vanishes
- sauce amount resets
- crumbs or garnish debris teleport
- tool angle changes and no longer matches the result
