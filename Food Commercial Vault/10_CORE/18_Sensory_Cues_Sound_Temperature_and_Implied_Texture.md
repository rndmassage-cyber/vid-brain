# Sensory Cues, Sound, Temperature and Implied Texture

## Why This Matters
Many great food ads work even on mute because the image itself suggests sound, heat, pressure, and texture.

## Non-Negotiable Rules
- The viewer should be able to infer the food texture visually.
- Heat must be shown, not merely assumed.
- Thick and thin liquids should look obviously different.
- Crunch needs debris, fracture, or micro-contrast.
- Sound is optional; implied sound is not.

## Heat and Steam Perception
Hot food becomes more believable when at least two of these are present:
- visible steam
- softened glossy fat or cheese
- bubbling edge activity
- utensils lifting hot contents
- atmospheric backlight
- fresh cut or break with escaping moisture

Avoid hot food that looks room-temperature and dry.

## Crunch Cues
Crunch is sold visually through:
- rigid broken edges
- crumbs or flakes
- fracture lines
- micro-contrast
- sudden release motion
- interior contrast against a crisp shell

A fried or baked item without fracture cues often feels stale.

## Sizzle Cues
Sizzle is suggested by:
- tiny hot bubbles
- shimmering oil
- aggressive edge activity on hot surfaces
- fast smoke wisps or steam ribbons
- glistening heat-exposed surfaces

## Thick vs Thin Sauce Perception
### Thick
Looks like:
- slower movement
- edge cling
- broader glossy rolls
- delayed drip
- visible mass

### Thin
Looks like:
- faster spread
- lower edge buildup
- quicker pooling
- finer droplets

The viewer should never be unsure whether a sauce is heavy, creamy, sticky, brothy, or watery.

## Visual ASMR Rules
Use visual cues that make the viewer mentally hear:
- crunch
- snap
- hiss
- sizzle
- spoon tap
- fizz
- sticky peel
- glaze drag

Do not overdo it until every shot becomes noisy or gimmicky.

## When Sound Helps
Sound helps most when the food action contains:
- fracture
- bubbling
- pouring
- crackle
- scraping
- frying
- carbonation
- ice contact

## When Sound Is Optional
Sound can remain secondary when:
- the ad relies on beauty and heat cues more than event cues
- the final output may be watched muted
- the visual texture already communicates the tactile result clearly

## What Makes Texture Feel Real
- consistent surface logic
- reaction to force
- aftermath
- contrast between outer shell and interior
- plausible liquid thickness
- temperature cues that match the dish

## What Makes Texture Feel Dead
- smooth uniform surfaces where irregularity should exist
- no debris on crunchy actions
- no drip behavior on wet actions
- no steam on hot servings
- no visible resistance during interaction
