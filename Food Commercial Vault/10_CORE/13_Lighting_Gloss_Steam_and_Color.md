# Lighting, Gloss, Steam and Color

## Why This Matters
Light does not simply illuminate food.  
It defines whether the dish feels juicy or dry, fresh or stale, premium or cheap.

## Non-Negotiable Rules
- Avoid flat frontal lighting for hero food shots.
- Use backlight or side-backlight when steam, translucency, gloss, or edge definition matters.
- Use negative fill to create sculptural depth.
- Match the reflection style to the food texture.
- Warmth should feel appetizing, not orange and fake.
- Background and plate colors must support the food, not neutralize it.

## Backlight and Edge Light
Backlight is the default premium food-lighting logic because it:
- reveals steam
- lights translucent leaves and edges
- makes liquids glow
- creates dimensional contour
- increases separation from the background

Use edge light when you want:
- a glossy rim on sauces
- contour on stacked foods
- clean vessel separation
- lifted shape from dark environments

## Steam Visibility Rules
Steam is invisible without the right conditions.
To show heat:
- place the light behind or slightly behind the steam
- use a darker or mid-tone background where possible
- keep the steam localized near the food source
- show movement, not static fog

Weak steam logic makes hot food feel lukewarm.

## Gloss and Specular Highlight Control
Gloss must be intentional.
### Use sharper highlights for:
- crispy crust peaks
- oily grill marks
- splashes
- droplets
- shiny cut fruit
- tiny fat bubbles

### Use broader highlights for:
- broths
- chocolate glaze
- melted cheese sheets
- glassware
- creamy sauces
- desserts with polished surfaces

Gloss should describe texture, not flatten it.

## Hard vs Soft Reflection Strategy
### Harder light
Best when you need:
- crisp fracture
- rugged crust
- sparkle on droplets
- aggressive micro-contrast
- visible grain or crackle

### Softer light
Best when you need:
- luxury smoothness
- large rolling reflections
- beauty on soups, glazes, drinks, and curved surfaces
- premium wraparound glow

## Negative Fill and Depth
Without shadow control, food becomes flat.
Use negative fill logic to:
- deepen bun creases
- shape crust cavities
- define meat ridges
- keep bowls and plates from blending into the table

The goal is sculpted appetite, not murky darkness.

## Warmth vs Realism
Warmth usually helps appetite, but it must remain believable.
Use warm cues in:
- highlights
- reflected sauce glow
- roasted browns
- crust color
- dining-room ambience

Preserve realism by keeping:
- greens clean, not yellow
- whites neutral enough to read as food-safe
- reds rich, not neon
- shadows cool only when they increase separation, not spoil freshness

## Background and Plate Color Interaction
The environment changes food perception.
General rules:
- warm food often benefits from cooler, darker surroundings
- fresh green food benefits from clean neutrals or deep contrast
- white plates increase cleanliness but can flatten pale foods
- black or dark plateware can make gloss pop but may hide dark sauces
- heavily patterned surfaces compete with detailed textures

The dish should dominate the color conversation.

## What Makes It Look Expensive
- controlled reflections
- appetizing warmth
- clear separation
- believable steam
- dimensional shadows
- a background that supports, not distracts
- color contrast that increases food visibility

## What Makes It Look Flat or Plastic
- front light
- identical shine on every surface
- no shadow modeling
- steam against a bright washed-out background
- over-saturated fake warmth
- color palette that turns ingredients muddy

## Decision Rules
Ask:
1. Which surface should glow?
2. Which surface should stay more matte?
3. Where should the steam read best?
4. Does the dish pop from the environment?
5. Do the highlights describe real texture?
