# Visual Deliciousness and Appetite Principles

## Why This Matters
A food ad succeeds only when the viewer subconsciously believes the dish will feel good to eat right now.
Visual deliciousness is the set of cues that trigger that belief.

## Non-Negotiable Rules
- The food must look **ready to eat**.
- The hero textures must be immediately legible.
- The dish must appear fresh, not tired.
- The food should suggest temperature when temperature matters.
- The viewer must be able to imagine the first bite, sip, scoop, or pull.

## Readiness-to-Eat Perception
The most persuasive food is visually accessible.
Examples:
- a burger with a clean bite path
- noodles already being lifted
- a spoon entering soup
- a pizza slice already separated enough to grab
- a dessert with a spoon crack or cream reveal

Avoid states that feel unfinished or awkward:
- ingredients that look unassembled
- garnish blocking the main texture
- unmotivated top-down clutter
- closed or inaccessible food forms

## Freshness Cues
Freshness is communicated by:
- hydrated surfaces
- crisp edges
- clean color separation
- visible surface life
- believable shine where the food should be moist
- absence of grey, stale, wilted, or muddy tones

Freshness dies when:
- greens go yellow
- sauces lose reflection
- bread looks dusty or flat
- cut faces look oxidized
- the dish feels untouched by heat or moisture where heat or moisture should exist

## Gloss and Juiciness
Gloss signals life.
Not every surface should be equally shiny, but the right ones must reflect light:
- meat juices
- sauces
- melted cheese
- broths
- fruit surfaces
- glazes
- cold condensation

Too little gloss makes food feel dry.  
Too much uniform gloss makes it feel plastic.

## Temperature Cues
Heat and cold must be visually suggested.
Hot cues:
- backlit steam
- rolling micro-bubbles
- softened edges in fat or cheese
- rising aroma-like movement
- fresh cut surfaces releasing moisture

Cold cues:
- condensation
- frosted edges
- clean glass highlights
- dense, stable surfaces
- crisp, refreshing color palette

## Dynamic Presentation
The food should rarely feel inert.
Dynamic presentation can come from:
- falling garnish
- movement of sauce
- bubbling
- rising steam
- a hand approaching
- a cut beginning
- a surface being disturbed

Even a finished plate should feel like something is about to happen or has just happened.

## Embodied Eating Simulation
The viewer should be able to imagine:
- the pressure needed to bite it
- the resistance of the crust
- the thickness of the sauce
- the weight of the spoonful
- the stretch of the cheese
- the snap of a shell or crust

This imagined physicality is one of the strongest appetite triggers.

## What Increases Desire
- readiness-to-eat cues
- visible freshness
- selective gloss
- heat or cold cues
- tactile actions
- strong texture hierarchy
- controlled imperfection
- clean color separation
- visual access to the most edible portion

## What Kills Appetite
- flat front light
- muddy colors
- overly symmetrical sterile styling
- dead surfaces with no moisture logic
- unclear edible entry point
- cluttered garnish that hides the hero texture
- random motion with no appetite payoff

## Decision Rules
Ask these questions:
1. What is the first bite, sip, scoop, or pull?
2. What texture should the viewer most want to experience?
3. Does the shot reveal temperature?
4. Does the dish feel alive right now?
5. Does the composition invite eating or only observation?
